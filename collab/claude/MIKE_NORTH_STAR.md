## Who Mike Is

Builder-operator with enterprise-scale depth. Not a strategist. Not an advisor. The person who walks into a mess and makes it legible, structured, and functional — and leaves infrastructure that keeps working after he's gone.

His identity word is **clarity**. He has run 400-person global organizations (IQVIA Head of Global Financial Shared Services), built enterprise data platforms at Fortune 500 scale (VP Enterprise Data & Analytics), and designed automation COEs using Snowflake, Alteryx, and Python. He is a CPA with deep financial controls roots who has moved deliberately toward data, analytics, and transformation leadership. He built a 10-person analytics org across data engineering, reporting, and data science. His systems have had 6,800+ users and $318M in total views.

His own words on his value: *"My value is not projects — it's infrastructure that keeps working long after I leave the room."*

His four-step operating pattern: diagnose → design the system → build clarity → deliver measurable outcomes. Every case study is quantified: $10M savings, 30% headcount reduction, 88-91% model accuracy, $57M cost targets supported. He measures himself in scale.

## Career Arc

**EY** (audit, CPA foundation) → **KKR / Siguler Guff** (PE/finance) → **IQVIA** (7 years, Apr 2017–May 2024, VP level, Fortune 500, global scale) → **Markman Group** (own consulting firm, May 2024, closed to take Abivax) → **Abivax** (current, transition role, below his ceiling).

**Why he left IQVIA:** He built what he set out to build. Platform was stable and scaled. He wanted to move from optimizing inside a large machine to owning transformation end-to-end with direct accountability. After 7 years he had no patience left for Fortune 500 corporate politics across 90,000 employees — the bureaucracy, the pace, the distance between a decision and its execution. He wanted to move faster and have more direct impact. Thought he could do it independently.

**Why Markman Group failed:** Two clients, both disasters. Laurel AG started well — his primary contact was a former KKR colleague who understood the art of the possible and moved fast. Mike ramped a team and built two state-of-the-art applications. Halfway through, that contact left to go back to PE. The replacement CEO had no interest in tech and cut everything. Mike had to fight to get paid. Metropolitan Partners was worse: his contact (another former colleague, newly hired to build data infrastructure) was submarined by internal politics from day one. Mike spent significant time and effort on a diagnostic engagement he loathed, working for people he describes as political scum, and had to fight for final payment. Contact was fired after 6 months. No further work came. The consulting dream — real impact at multiple companies — turned out to be a dead end. The reality was chasing payment, managing consultants he increasingly questioned, and watching key relationships collapse through no fault of his own. He came out of it demoralized and financially depleted. He needed to get back inside.

**The pattern to understand:** Both consulting failures shared the same structure — a trusted, capable primary contact who lost their position or authority mid-engagement. Mike does his best work when the person at the top is capable, stable, and has real organizational power. When that person disappears, everything collapses. This is not just a consulting problem — it applies to Abivax too. His success here is dependent on Hema and Didier remaining stable sponsors through implementation.

## What He Needs to Do His Best Work

Based on the Markman Group experience and IQVIA frustration, the conditions that matter:

- **A capable, stable, empowered sponsor at the top.** Both consulting disasters collapsed when the key contact lost authority. He needs a sponsor with real organizational power who isn't going anywhere.
- **Scale that matches his ceiling.** He measures himself in organizational scale. Small teams and low volumes are demoralizing over time regardless of how interesting the work is.
- **Speed and decision velocity.** The primary reason he left IQVIA was the pace of a 90,000-person machine. He needs an environment where he can make decisions and see them implemented — not fight politics for months.
- **People who are worth working for.** He loathed the Metropolitan Partners engagement because the people were, in his words, scum. He has a very low tolerance for political actors who obstruct rather than build. He will disengage when surrounded by them.
- **Real equity on the table.** The consulting chapter showed that billing-based compensation without equity upside is not satisfying even when the work is good. He needs skin in the game.
